# Prologue

Listen, O my son, to the precepts of thy master, and incline the ear of thy heart, and cheerfully receive and faithfully execute the admonitions of thy loving Father, that by the toil of obedience thou mayest return to Him from whom by the sloth of disobedience thou hast gone away.

To thee, therefore, my speech is now directed, who, giving up thine own will, takest up the strong and most excellent arms of obedience, to do battle for Christ the Lord, the true King.

# Chapter 1: Of the Kinds or the Life of Monks

It is well known that there are four kinds of monks. The first are the Cenobites: those who live in monasteries and serve under a rule and an Abbot.

The second are the Anchorites or Hermits: those who, no longer in the first fervour of their conversion, but after long probation in a monastery, having learned by the help of many brethren to fight against the devil, go out well armed from the ranks of the community to the solitary combat of the desert. They are able now, with no help save from God, to fight against the vices of the flesh and their own evil thoughts.

The third kind of monks is that of Sarabaites, the most detestable kind. These, not having been tested, as gold in the furnace, by any rule or by the lessons of experience, are as soft as lead, and still in their works keep faith with the world. They are known by their tonsure to lie to God. These in pairs or in small companies, without a shepherd, keep for themselves, not for the Lord, their own sheepfolds. Their law is the satisfaction of their desires: whatever they think of or choose, that they call holy; what they like not, that they consider unlawful.

The fourth kind of monks are those called Gyrovagues. These spend their whole lives tramping from province to province, staying as guests for three or four days at a time in different monasteries. Always on the move, they never settle down, and are slaves to their own wills and the allurements of gluttony. In every way they are worse than the Sarabaites.

Of the most miserable conduct of all these it is better to be silent than to speak. Leaving them therefore aside, let us proceed, with the help of God, to treat of the strongest kind of monks, the Cenobites.

# Chapter 2: What Kind of Man the Abbot Ought to Be

An Abbot who is worthy to rule a monastery ought always to remember what he is called, and fulfil in his deeds the name of a Superior. For he is believed to hold the place of Christ in the monastery, when he is called by His name, according to the saying of the Apostle: "You have received the spirit of adoption of sons, whereby we cry, Abba, Father."

Therefore the Abbot ought not to teach or ordain or command anything which is against the law of the Lord; but his commands and his teaching should be infused into the minds of his disciples like the leaven of divine justice.

# Chapter 3: Of Calling the Brethren to Council

Whenever any important business has to be done in the monastery, let the Abbot call together the whole community, and himself set forth the matter. Having heard the counsel of the brethren, let him consider it within himself, and then do what he shall judge most expedient. We have said that all should be called to council, because it is often to a younger brother that the Lord reveals what is best. Let the brethren give their advice with all humble subjection, and not presume stubbornly to defend their own opinions. Let the decision rather depend on the Abbot's judgment, and all submit to whatever he shall decide is best. Yet just as it is proper for disciples to obey their master, so also it is his to dispose all things with prudence and justice.
